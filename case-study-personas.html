Project Overview

In this case study I explore how storytelling techniques—like character creation and scene‐by‐scene storyboarding—can deepen our empathy for real users of public transit ticket kiosks. By observing, interviewing, and mapping narratives, I crafted interfaces that read like intuitive stories rather than cold menus.

Observations

On-site field observations revealed distinct “characters” in the kiosk story:

  • Speed‐runners: City natives who zip through options— they need shortcuts and pre-saved trips.
  • Cautious newcomers: Tourists who hesitate at each tap—clear labels and reassurance are key.
  • Hygiene-mindful: Many users avoided direct screen touches, suggesting a side-panel stylus or voice command alternative.
  • Tap-to-pay hopefuls: Users expected NFC payment and were surprised when only cash or chip cards worked—indicating a mismatch in expectations.

Interview Insights

One-on-one interviews uncovered narrative beats for each persona’s journey:

  1. “How could this go faster?” “Ask me my destination once, then show the cheapest routes.”
  2. “Did you prepare before using it?” “No—I rely on my subway experience elsewhere.”
  3. “Why digital over in-person?” “Less awkward than talking to someone, and no language barrier.”

Reflection

Treating interface design like filmmaking—casting personas, storyboarding scenes, and iterating each beat—made the final product feel intuitive for everyone. This narrative lens will guide all my future UX work, ensuring every click carries meaning and every screen advances the story.
